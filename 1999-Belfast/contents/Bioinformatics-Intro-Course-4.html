4 INTRODUCTORY SEQUENCE ANALYSIS

The aim of this module is to explore some typical bioinformatics tools. These will be applied to simple DNA sequence analysis procedures. We'll taste some good old fashioned 'command line' programs as well as some more user friendly applications running behind 'graphical user interfaces'. By the end of this session, you should deplore but appreciate why 'user unfriendly' programs are still in use...

4.1 INTRODUCTION

4.1.1 How does bioinformatics assist investigators in gene discovery ?

The crucial need in WW2 to 'crack' secret codes led to the formidable development of computers and information technology (IT). The curious analogy between the computer binary code and the DNA four base code is at the heart of the involvement of IT in molecular biology (see Richard Dawkins, A river out of Eden). With the exponential amount of genetic code becoming available, it comes as no surprise that bioinformatics has taken such a predominant role in 'cracking' the code of life: during 1997, the combined world human sequencing centres produced on average 8 kilobases of genomic sequence per hour, 24 hours a day! For the latest estimates, check out the Genome MOT ( http://www.ebi.ac.uk/~sterk/genome-MOT/).The ease with which computers are capable of processing raw indigestible nucleic acid sequences into human understandable information (most of the time!), has made bioinformatics as indispensable in the lab as the Gilson and the Eppendorf. Indeed, there would be little hope of deciphering the 300,000,000 bases (give or take a few millions) of the human genome without the help of computer software.There are numerous experimental approaches to the identification and analysis of novel nucleic acid sequences. These usually fall into one of the two following general categories:
  1. top down strategy : you know the phenotype, you're after the encoding gene(s)
  2. bottom up strategy : you have the gene, you're wondering what's its phenotype could be
Depending on the specific research strategy, bioinformatics will be routinely required during experimental research to :
  • Carry out linkage analysis (e.g. deduce location of disease loci from pedigrees)
  • Gather raw DNA sequence data (e.g. contig assembly, library analysis)
  • Prepare cloning intensive experiments (e.g. knock-outs, gene therapy)
  • Design diagnosis tools (e.g. sequence alignment, restriction patterns, PCR)
  • Extract encoded information (e.g. promoters, responsive elements, exons and gene structures)
Figure: An overview chart to show some broad uses of bioinformatics.Computational tools also intrude into other aspects of biology, namely database searching (covered tomorrow morning) and protein analysis (covered Wednesday). You can find out more about the bioinformatics tools available in the tutorial "I have a sequence...":http://www.hgmp.mrc.ac.uk/Courses/I_Have_A_Sequence/We will skip over the hugely complex linkage analysis and the highly technical raw DNA data collection which are well out of the scope of this course (both are covered in their own specialised HGMP courses), and look at tools that illustrate the last three techniques.To begin with, let's experiment with a few 'simple' nucleic acid analysis tools that are used routinely at the bench to :
  • look for potential splice sites in a genomic DNA sequence (discover computer generated false positives!)
  • compare two different DNA sequences to each other (pairwise sequence alignments)
  • detect restriction enzymes cut sites (restriction mapping)
  • help in the judicious design of primers for the polymerase chain reaction (PCR)

4.2 PRACTICAL (CASE STUDY)

Let’s imagine that ...you are doing research on cardiac actin in Xenopus laevis (toad). You have screened a cDNA library for a clone expressing actin and have sequenced it. You are now interested in comparing it to the genomic sequence to find out where the splice sites are. To do this, you could align your sequence to to the genomic fragment where the gene originated from. You know from the literature that the genomic sequence of the actin gene is available in the EMBL sequence database under ID ‘ xlactcag’. We will use this information later in the practical.Objectives of this exercise include:
  • To demonstrate how the computer can be used to compute meaningful alignments between pairs of related sequences.
  • To illustrate the weakness of such methods and to emphasise the need for careful interpretation of computer generated alignments.
  • To illustrate the use of graphically previewing the similarities between two sequences before computing a textual alignment.
  • To show another instance where a good GUI can make programs far easier and quicker to use than from the command line.
During the course of this afternoon you will carry out several analyses on this mock sequence run, first using command line programs and then more comfortably behind graphical user interfaces.

4.4.4 Restriction mapping (GCG MAP)

If you look closely at the alignment produced by bestfit[5] or gap you can see that the alignment is not perfect. Between bases 861 and 1041 there is a mismatch of two bases with the genomic fragment. You could have known that the match was not exact from some other part of the information on alignment also....Do you know what? (Hint....Percentage).It is possible that you made a sequencing error (can you think of any other reasons ? How sure are we that the genomic fragment is the source of the cDNA clone ?) and to investigate this, you choose not to sequence the cDNA again, but use two different methods. First we are going to try restriction mapping and then we will amplify the region and sequence the PCR product, to check which of the sequences is incorrect (or if both are correct, we might have found a mutant/variant).

How do we find suitable restriction sites?

Several hundred restriction enzymes (RE) have been isolated (mostly from bacteria) and their nucleotide recognition site characterised. The motifs recognised by RE are mostly palindromes and vary in length from DNA tetramers (frequent cutters) to 26-mers (BaeI). An updated public database of the current available RE (Rebase) is used to scan the DNA sequence under examination. The restriction map thus produced can be used to design a "digest" pattern.

Practical

4.4.5 PCR primer design (GCG PRIMER)

Why ?

The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) has been to molecular biology what the invention of the motor engine was to terrestrial travel : it's made things a lot faster (but a bit more dirty too...). Designing oligos for PCR can be done by hand, but experience shows that you can save time, aggravation & funds by using a computer to avoid bad primers. Bad primers pairs have melting temperatures that are too different, are not specific enough to your target, hybridise together or form internal hairpin loops, amongst other things.One further advantage of using bioinformatics to help in designing PCR primers, is that algorithms usually use more accurate methods for T m calculations than the rule of thumb used at the bench : (A+T) = 2 and (G+C) = 4 degrees centigrade.

How ?

If you wish to find out more about the way computers approach primer design, look through the "Help" offered in the Prime program of the GCG package that we will use for the practical. You will find such riveting information as thermodynamic calculations of the T mp = delta H / (delta S + R x ln(c/4)) - 273.15 + 16.6 x log[K(+)] where delta H is the enthalpy of helix formation...

Practical

You would like to amplify the region containing the discrepancy and then directly sequence the PCR product to resolve the matter once and for all.Primer design : GCG’s PRIME

4.4.6 Scanning a database for a primer (GCG FINDPATTERN)

Why ?

It is handy to check your prospective oligos to be used in PCR's against currently known sequences in databases in order to avoid potential non specific hybridisation and possible amplification of the wrong fragment. Some www servers are starting to experiment with 'virtual PCR', but it is still too slow to carry out here.

How ?

The GCG program findpatterns simply slides a short sequence pattern (in this case your input oligo) against a list of sequences (here the sequences in a subset of the GenBank and EMBL databases). You can define the patterns ambiguously and allow mismatches. It is this little program that is also used internally by GCG's MAP to locate restriction enzyme sites.

4.5 CONCLUSION

4.5.1 Why bother at all with command line ?

All the arguments for and against command line should have become apparent in the previous practicals. Summarised, they look like this :Command Line

For

  • Can be accessed through non graphical terminals (hopefully a rare occurrence today) or slow networks (not so rare !)
  • Can be quicker for simple jobs or when you become an experienced user
  • Consistent interface (often several GUI's are created for the same program, each GUI will look completely different to each other)
  • Programs can be called from inside scripts (i.e. very simple programs that call other programs to automate tedious repetitive tasks, such as periodically searching a database, or dealing with large sequencing projects with lots of sequence files)
  • Full functionality

Against

  • Slow learning curve !
  • Where's the help ?
  • What is the syntax?
  • What does the output mean?
  • Output in text format limits the readability of results
 Graphical (GUI)

For

  • Intuitive ! Fast learning curve!
  • Online help
  • Visible options (check boxes, forms etc.)
  • Less typing
  • Sometimes graphical output can help interpret results (e.g. graph vs list of numbers)
  • Project input / output files management

Against

  • Sometimes limited functionality
  • Inconsistent look and feel for the same underlying programs
In a word, it is still worth learning to master command line program invocation. This might change when there is enough left-over time and funding from core algorithm development to be channelled into improving scientific software interfaces... Already the world wide web is offering 'form' interfaces to many bioinformatics programs: fill out & submit.
